1. What is it that we decided to do?

Development of the CLIx mathematics modules were guided by the broad CLIx objective of introducing an innovative learning space for students in Govt. schools with meaningful use of technology, the following steps helped us trace ways forward:

  • CLIx Mathematics Team started by identifying the topics in high school mathematics curriculum that are often perceived as challenging by the learners. This task was done by exploring and reviewing the existing studies and reports.
  • We explored topics that covered major portion in the high school mathematics curriculum. This was done by reviewing the curriculum and textbooks used in different states.
  • We conducted informal consultation and discussion with teachers to ascertain topics that are seen as challenging to teach using the existing teaching resources.
  • We identified areas where technology affordances could be leveraged for meaningful use leading to effective learning enhancement. We also identified the difficult components of content which needed the extra affordances provided by technology, for example, content that needs help in visualization, that needs digital construction, that allows iterating. Thus, leveraging the use of technology.

2. How did we identify what is to be done?

Our approach was to strengthen foundational topic-areas which are crucial for understanding high school mathematics. Developing geometric reasoning was one of them. Other important areas are understanding multiplicative reasoning and understanding equations. It is commonly observed that Geometry chapters are often dealt with towards the end of the school academic year thereby reducing the amount of time and focus available for this theme in general (on account of pressure to complete the syllabus and summative assessments). Hence, a dedicated focus on geometric reasoning made sense from a practical perspective as well. Similarly, strengthening of proportional reasoning and its varied applications and making sense of linear equations are the other key areas of school mathematics which are often not thoroughly built because of which understanding of further topic-areas are impeded. Therefore, CLIx Mathematics modules attempted to build foundational understanding in these areas by making meaningful integration of technology with classroom pedagogy. At the outset, following actions points were taken up:

  • Textbook analysis of high school mathematics curriculum on what topics were covered in school geometry - although development of foundational understanding of concepts, deductive reasoning, proofs and their construction were the long term goals of teaching geometry, often these notions and skills were not fully emphasised or developed.
  • Review of research papers indicated learners’ difficulties and errors and ways to tackle them.
  • Interaction with teachers in planned workshops and informal interactions with them highlighted the practical problems of time availability, diversity in classrooms, language, access, learners’ basic preparedness and so on.

3. What informed our approach?

After identifying the mathematical topic-areas to work on, a detailed review of the research literature was done which along with the following approach informed the development of CLIx Mathematics modules.

Design Thinking

The existing research in Geometry and our understanding of the learning gaps informed our approach. We decided to start with basic concepts in geometry and then gradually move towards justification and proofs which are higher order skills. It was decided to adopt a blended approach of both hands-on as well as digital tasks in the mathematics modules. Design thinking of the digital game and digital tasks was initiated at a design camp which was conducted at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT), USA in August 2015 for preparing prototypes and models. These prototypes were used to conduct pilot trials with students to get feedback on the development of the digital content. This development activity was iterative in nature and feedback was received at various stages of development and changes were made. Hands-on and digital tasks design ensured to create opportunities for students for multiple explorations and attempts on their own. Additionally, to encourage collaborative learning design for buddy login, uploading of notes and discussion portals were worked out to encourage students to discuss among each other. Teacher’s role was envisaged as extremely significant in terms of facilitating the classroom discussion.

4. What informed our design?

We worked closely with the CLIx Technology Team and MIT Design Lab to understand the design possibilities and challenges for the content under development. Prototypes of the digital activities were tested with the students. Based on the feedback, together with the technology team, we developed digital prototypes iteratively and again tested those with the students. This Design Based Research (DBR) approach helped us in modifying our design to make it user-friendly. Understanding field realities also played an important role in informing our design approach. Understanding the size of the computer labs, electricity problems, and other logistical challenges, we decided to have digital as well as hands-on activities in our modules. Valuing the increasing multilingual character of the modern societies, our design approach made provisions for language toggling. Activities design was also done by keeping in mind language syntax in different languages and to facilitate ways which could have minimal impact during language translation.

5. What did we favour to be our design elements??

e.g., blended form, collaborative approach, making sure students have the autonomy, teacher's role

  • The math modules were conceptualised to have a blended form valuing both digital and hands-on activities which are seen as complements to each other. While hands-on activities in the classroom created scaffolds for artefact creation and their manipulation by hand, digital tasks in computer laboratory were aimed at providing multiple opportunities and ways of solving/doing tasks by allowing allow students to create, iterate and learn from their own mistakes.
  • Considering the small physical spaces available in the computer laboratories with limited available computer systems and valuing collaborative learning, task design accounted for doing digital activities in groups of 2-3 students. Such group work promoted by designing buddy logins valued learning from each other and learners’ autonomy. These aspects are part of the inherent design elements of the CLIx Mathematics modules.
  • Design elements Leveraging use of technologies which The design of the module considers the teacher to be an active participant in the classroom. Students’ mistakes provide teachers with the issues to be addressed and hence opens up the opportunity to have discussions in the classroom to arrive at a shared understanding for particular content.
  • The math modules imagine the classroom to be a space to engage in the mathematical processes of reasoning, justifying, arguing, creating one’s own definition and reaching a consensus by discussing with the pairs.

6. What was the process of ideation?

The process of ideation was an iterative process going back and forth on the design thinking as well taking cues from the research literature. The design ideas were also discussed with subject experts from HBCSE-TIFR, MIT, NIAS. There used to be weekly discussions over WebEx with experts from MIT on the design prototypes and furthering the plan. Moving ahead, piloting of design prototypes of the digital tools with selected students from schools in Mumbai helped the ideation process and changes were incorporated based on the review of the pilot studies.

7. What are the things we have learned through this process and how did it contribute to the process?

This process of content development brought many new learning opportunities for us. One of them is maintaining a balance between what do we want to put in the design from a content creators’ point of view and what can technology offer (understanding its limitations). This helped us to rethink our own visualization of the ideal content. Understanding ground realities helped us to strategize implementation by suggesting hands-on and digital activities to the teachers. We also created students and teachers workbooks so as to enable teachers to spend time in the computer lab when a few other students are sitting in the classroom engaged with the workbook activities.

8. How we tweaked the language and the content that went into the teacher's preparation work?

Language translations were prepared by using the exact or same Hindi/Telugu terminologies as used in the textbooks for a particular state. Proper nouns were used which would be broadly applicable to each state’s context. Workshops were conducted where school teachers participated for validation of language translation of the modules as well as of the teachers’ RTICT courses. Based on the suggestions, language use was tweaked.

9. What are the different artefacts that came out of the process?

        During the process of module development, the team created resources for students as well as for teachers. Student workbook was created for Geometric Reasoning and Proportional Reasoning module in Telugu, Hindi, and English. Teachers handbook was developed in Hindi and English in order to help teachers to facilitate the module content as well as give them opportunities to record their observations and reflections.

10. What did we imagine the teacher's role in this ??

In all the three Clix-Mathematics modules, teachers role is central and crucial to the entire process of implementation. The content of the modules was developed by keeping in mind that the students get enough opportunities on their own to explore but at the same time, the teacher plays the role of a facilitator, both in the computer lab as well as in the classroom. Teacher generates classroom discussions and presents challenging questions to the students. The teacher has the autonomy to use the existing tools to go beyond the given activities in the module content and explore other learning opportunities with students.

11. Also what is unique to our domain (Math domain) e.g game tool, activities, etc?

The unique feature of the math domain content is that it creates an opportunity for students to engage with the math content by providing realistic challenges or context. It allows students to do reasoning with their pair, teacher, and the whole class to arrive at a shared understanding of the mathematical concept. The games, interactives, digital and hands-on activities are designed in such a way that it allows students to learn through iteration and mistakes by having a discussion around it in the class. Along with the pre and post assessment questionnaire for each math module, each of the tools is also collecting a backend data of the user, which can be analysed and researched further to check the efficacy of the module and hence the learning.
